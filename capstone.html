Objective 3. Report interesting findings

Based on all the graphs produced, there are a few frequent words and then there is a steep drop in frequencies yielding a long tail of low-frequency words. Nonetheless, about half of the dataset is composed of only 200 words! The same can be said with the n-grams. However, the larger the n-gram, the steeper the decline in high frequency. This is expected since a group of words in a specific order will be less frequent than a single word.

Most of the high-frequency words are articles like “the” and prepositions like “of”. Combinations of these are the most common bigrams. Trigrams and 4-grams appear to be more dependant on the dataset (than the English language). The word ‘prom’ appears repeated many times in the dataset.You can see the top 20 words, bigrams, trigrams and 4-grams in the table below.

Objective 4. Receive feedback on plans to create a prediction algorithm

My idea to create the algorithm is to use the quanteda package as a way to clean and search from term frequency matrices. A word will the searched through a sorted list (to improve speed) based on the frequency. The algorithm will first search on a list of most frequent trigrams, if nothing is found, a bigram database will be searched. Finally, if nothing was found, the top pick of the most frequent words will be return based on what figure of speech was the input word. In other words, if the user types a noun, ‘car’ for example, a verb will be returned, such as ‘is’ and not another noun or pronoun, such as ‘I’. I decided not to exclude contractions from the dataset as they are commonly use in English. Similarly, if other language word is frequently used, I didn’t want to exclude it either. However, symbols, numbers and punctuation were excluded and will not be predicted.
